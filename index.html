VADER — Valence Aware Dictionary and sEntiment Reasoner — has no idea what a stock is, what "moon" means on Reddit, or that "no profit" is bad news. It just adds up a list of numbers. Here's the whole trick, laid bare.

02 — Under the hood

The "dictionary" is a real, static list

VADER's dictionary (its lexicon) is a list of about 7,500 words, each hand-rated by human annotators on a scale from roughly −4 (most negative) to +4 (most positive), before VADER ever sees your sentence. Nothing in it is learned or updated — it's fixed. Here's a small sample of real entries:

03 — Where it breaks

If a word isn't in the dictionary, it has no opinion

VADER's lexicon was built from general English writing — mostly product reviews, tweets, and news, rated years ago. It has never seen r/WallStreetBets. None of these words score anything, positive or negative, even though every one of them carries obvious sentiment on a finance forum:

This is exactly the pitfall from Section 4 of the notebook: VADER's dictionary isn't specialized for financial language, let alone social media slang. "Moon" and "diamond hands" don't register as positive. "Bear" and "bagholding" don't register as negative. VADER just sees empty space where the meaning is.

04 — Where it breaks, part 2

One word, two meanings — VADER only knows one

Dictionaries can't tell context apart. VADER scores the word beating as negative (−2.0) every single time, because that's the only score it's allowed to have. Watch what happens to two sentences that use it completely differently:

Google is beating all of its competitors this quarter.

A human reads this as good news for Google.

VADER says: NEGATIVE
Google's stock is really taking a beating this week.

A human reads this as bad news for Google.

VADER says: NEGATIVE

VADER gets the second one right by accident — not because it understood "taking a beating" as an idiom, but because the dictionary only ever has one answer for "beating," no matter what surrounds it.

05 — The verdict

So why use VADER at all?

A dictionary-and-rules approach is a real, useful tool — just not a magic one. Knowing exactly what it can and can't do is what makes it usable.

Strengths

  • Fast. Every score is just a dictionary lookup and a sum — no training, no GPU, milliseconds per sentence.
  • Transparent. You can point to the exact word that produced a score. There's no hidden reasoning to reverse-engineer.
  • Free and offline. No API calls, no cost per request, no rate limits.

Weaknesses

  • No context. Word order, sarcasm, and idioms don't change the score.
  • Fixed vocabulary. Slang, jargon, and new words (like "diamond hands") score as nothing at all.
  • One word, one meaning. "Beating" can't mean two different things depending on the sentence.
